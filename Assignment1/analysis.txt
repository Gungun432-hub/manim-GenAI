Pythagoras Theorem Animation

The animation successfully illustrates a right-angled triangle with squares constructed on each of its sides and clearly presents the relationship (a^2 + b^2 = c^2). However, it stops short of providing a rigorous geometric proof of the theorem. While the areas of the squares are displayed, they are not rearranged, decomposed, or transformed to visually demonstrate why the combined area of the two smaller squares equals that of the larger square. Instead, the smaller squares simply disappear and are replaced by the larger square, which weakens the explanatory value of the proof.

Additionally, the significance of the right angle is not consistently emphasized throughout the animation, making it less evident that the theorem applies specifically to right-angled triangles. Although different colours are assigned to the three squares, their purpose and interpretation are not explicitly explained, reducing the effectiveness of the visual communication. The transition from the area-based illustration to the final mathematical equation also feels somewhat abrupt and could be improved with intermediate explanatory steps to create a smoother conceptual flow.

Fourier Series Animation

The Fourier series animation effectively demonstrates how successive odd harmonics combine to approximate a square wave. The step-by-step addition of harmonic components provides a clear visual representation of the convergence process and helps viewers understand the fundamental idea behind Fourier synthesis.

However, several aspects of the presentation could be refined to improve readability. The axis labels and numerical markings are not always easy to interpret, particularly when multiple waveforms are displayed simultaneously. As additional harmonics are introduced, the cumulative-sum labels become increasingly lengthy, resulting in unnecessary visual clutter. Some annotations and legends also compete for space with the plotted curves, making the display appear crowded at certain stages.

Furthermore, the final approximation curve is not visually distinguished enough from the individual harmonic components, making it harder for viewers to identify the key result of the animation. The educational impact could also be enhanced by including brief explanatory notes describing why only odd harmonics are used and how each newly added harmonic contributes to a more accurate approximation of the square wave. Such additions would make the animation more accessible to audiences who may be unfamiliar with Fourier series concepts.
